The Water Cycle

The water cycle, also known as the hydrological cycle, describes the continuous movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth.

Key Stages:
1. Evaporation - The sun heats surface water in oceans, lakes, and rivers, turning it into water vapor that rises into the atmosphere.
2. Condensation - As water vapor rises and cools, it condenses into tiny water droplets forming clouds and fog.
3. Precipitation - Water falls from clouds as rain, snow, sleet, or hail depending on temperature conditions.
4. Collection - Water collects in oceans, rivers, lakes, and groundwater reservoirs, and the cycle begins again.
5. Transpiration - Plants release water vapor through their leaves, contributing to evaporation.

Importance:
- The water cycle distributes fresh water across the planet.
- It regulates Earth's temperature through the transfer of heat energy.
- It supports all life on Earth by providing fresh water for drinking, agriculture, and ecosystems.
- It drives weather patterns and climate systems globally.

The water cycle is powered primarily by solar energy and gravity.
